**A Chinese PhD Applicant**

# A PhD Dream Deferred: One Year of Silence from Canada's Immigration System

I am an ordinary young woman from China with a simple dream: to pursue a **PhD in Computer Science** and dedicate my life to research and learning.

I have no criminal record. I have no background that would give authorities reason for concern. I am simply a student who worked hard, earned admission to a Canadian university, and has been waiting ever since.

To prepare for my doctoral studies, I had already taken **one gap year** before applying. I accepted that sacrifice willingly. What I never anticipated was a **second year lost entirely to administrative silence**.

I submitted my **Canadian study permit application on March 5, 2025**. At first, the process moved quickly. My biometrics were acknowledged the same day and cleared by **March 7**. By late March, a re-examination was triggered and I was asked to complete a medical exam. I attended the exam on **April 8** and passed on **April 12**. Everything suggested that the application was progressing normally.

Then, on **April 10**, my application entered **security screening**.

I confirmed this through the application tracker’s backend code, where the **security flag appeared as code 17**. From that moment onward, the process fell completely silent.

On **April 11**, I submitted an **Access to Information request (ATIP)**, which returned no meaningful result. On **April 23**, I sent a webform inquiry and received only a **generic template response** five days later.

On **May 2**, my ATIP result officially confirmed what I had already feared: my application was in **security screening**, with **no timeline, no explanation, and no indication of when the process might end**.

Two years of my life are now on hold.

My academic future is suspended before it has even begun.

But the burden of this wait is not mine alone.

My **85-year-old grandfather** suffers from hypertension and diabetes. My **75-year-old grandmother** has begun showing early signs of **Alzheimer’s disease**. Every passing day is a day I may never get back with her.

I think about the limited time we may still have together—the memories she is slowly losing—and my hope that she will still recognize my face the day I finally have something to celebrate.

I wanted her to feel proud of me while she still could. That hope is what keeps me going, even as the waiting slowly erodes it.

I still remember the joy I felt the day my **PhD offer** arrived.

That joy has since faded into silence.

What remains is a kind of numbness—the exhaustion of waiting for a system that does not respond, of sending inquiries into what feels like a void, and of watching time pass while life stands still.

I am not a threat.

I am a student.

I am a granddaughter.

I have a university program waiting for me and grandparents growing older without answers.

I am not asking for special treatment. I ask only for **transparency**, a **reasonable timeline**, and the basic dignity of being treated as a person rather than a file left waiting in the dark.
