## My Story — Waiting for a Security Screening With No End

Lily, PGP Program, Edmonton, AB. Have waited for 40 months.

I immigrated to Canada in 2010 and I'm a senior professional educator. For the past 14 years, I have served as the Executive Director at a prestigious, large non-profit early childhood education center with a 53-year history, contributing to our community's families and schools, particularly in supporting special needs children. My husband is also a highly educated professional and technical expert. Our son completed his secondary and university education in Canada and now works as a Senior Data Analyst for the local school board. Our entire family consists of skilled professionals who contribute significantly to the Canadian government each year through high income taxes, consumption taxes, and property taxes, thereby supporting Canada's education system and economic development.

In 2020, I applied to sponsor my parents under the Parents and Grandparents Program (PGP). I was fortunate to be selected in the 2022 lottery and submitted their online application in November 2022. Unfortunately, for reasons unknown, my parents' case was transferred to the Vancouver Immigration Office (renowned for being the slowest processing office) for further processing on March 9, 2023. According to the IRCC website, the processing time for the PGP program is 24 months. However, based on information shared within our 2022 PGP applicants’ group, most cases were approved in less than 12 months by the Case Processing Centre in Mississauga. Regrettably, my parents' case received no updates for 14 months.

On May 8, 2024, we finally received a request for a medical exam and police clearance certificates. My parents complied immediately, and their online account subsequently showed these requirements as passed, and they received an Invitation to Pre‑arrival Services letter on July 30, 2024. Based on the shared experiences from our 2022 applicants’ group, this letter typically precedes final approval, but that didn't happen. Following group advice, I applied for IRCC ATIP notes on September 6, 2024, and received the file information on October 7, 2024.

Upon reviewing the records and officer notes from the ATIP, I was deeply frustrated to discover that my parents' application had only just entered the security screening phase. The 24‑month processing timeframe had already passed, yet the case was merely at the starting point of security screening, whose processing time is known to be extremely long, often lasting many years. I sought assistance from our Member of Parliament, who inquired with IRCC about the delay in December 2024. IRCC's response was that the standard processing time does not apply to a “special case” who needs security screening – a reply that offers no meaningful explanation or solution.

To this day, we have waited for 40 months. There has been no update or feedback on the security screening. It looks as though the case has entered a spacetime black hole. Furthermore, the ATIP information revealed numerous basic errors and logical inconsistencies, leading me to seriously question the diligence and professionalism of the IRCC officers involved. This raises the question: what accountability exists for the consequences faced by applicants due to such negligence and inaction?

Given the already extensive wait, IRCC suggested my parents apply for a Super Visa. However, the Super Visa approval process involves the same security screening as the PGP, essentially requiring us to start the waiting game anew. We have already waited 40 months. How much longer must we wait for our family reunion?

Due to this indefinite security screening, my parents are unable to obtain permanent residence or any visa, rendering them unable to enter Canada. Our only option is to frequently take leave from our jobs in Canada to visit them in our home country. This situation has had an extremely negative impact on our careers and life in Canada, and has created a significant financial burden.

Caring for our parents is a fundamental virtue and cultural cornerstone in Chinese tradition. As the saying goes, \"The tree desires stillness, but the wind will not cease; the child wishes to care for their parents, but they are no longer there.\" As time passes and my parents grow older, the inability to be with them, the guilt of not providing care, and the worry over their loneliness and well-being have plunged me into deep anxiety, even necessitating professional psychological support.

I chose to come to Canada, to work and build a life here, because of the values of humanitarianism, freedom, democracy, equality, and compassion that this country espouses. I wanted to contribute my efforts to building a better Canada. Now, however, I find myself questioning that initial choice. The indefinite security screening by IRCC is creating a humanitarian crisis, causing countless families like mine to endure the pain of prolonged separation.

If this security screening continues without an end in sight, I will be forced to consider abandoning the career I have worked so hard to build in Canada, selling our home, and leaving behind my career, my life, my friends – everything – to return to my home country and reunite with my family. Because in this world, nothing is more important than being together with family.

I am not alone; many other permanent residence applicants trapped in prolonged separation from their loved ones share this contemplation of giving up on Canada and returning home. If IRCC's inaction pushes us to this point, it will not only be a personal loss for us but also a tremendous loss for Canada.

It is well-known that Chinese immigrant applicants are typically highly educated, skilled professionals who contribute across various sectors of the Canadian economy. They enjoy stable incomes, own homes and vehicles, and annually contribute substantial income, consumption, and property taxes. These talented individuals form a vital part of Canada's tax base, labor force, consumer market, and real estate market. Should a significant number of them choose to leave Canada, the negative impact on taxation, the labor market, consumption, and housing would be substantial and should not be underestimated.

We understand, respect, and support the necessity of security screening. However, I am curious about the reasons the immigration authorities have for finding it necessary to conduct an in-depth and lengthy security screen for a case involving a daughter sponsoring her elderly parents, both over 80 years old, for family reunification. This sponsorship is based on her family's 16 years of outstanding career, income, tax payment records, a clean criminal record, and good social credit and reputation in Canada. How “special” is this case? What threat do either the exemplary sponsor or the elderly applicants pose to Canada's national security?

If a security screen is indeed necessary, there should be a reasonable processing timeframe. Elderly parents should not be made to wait indefinitely. Some applicants have passed away during these long waits, never to be reunited with their families. This is a humanitarian tragedy caused by IRCC's inaction.

If the immigration authorities are conducting such indiscriminate, large-scale, in-depth, and indefinite security reviews for PR and visa applicants from a specific ethnic group, I believe this constitutes blatant racial discrimination, an abuse of administrative power, and a waste of national resources, essentially amounting to governmental corruption. We have the right to protest against this.

Security screening must be applied equitably. Reports suggest that other ethnicities are not subjected to such treatment. Therefore, we demand the establishment of a security screening system that ensures procedural fairness, clear timelines, transparent communication, and mechanisms for oversight and accountability. Such a system is necessary to enable the timely completion of all backlogged and delayed PR and visa applications.
