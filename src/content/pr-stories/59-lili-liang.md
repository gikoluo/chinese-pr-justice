I submitted my study permit application on May 1, 2025, for a Master's program in Design at the Centre for Digital Media in Vancouver. In early June, my file entered security screening. In July, I completed a secondary mandatory review — what applicants in our community call "强二" — and I have heard nothing since.

I set out to study design because I believed in social responsibility. Instead, before I had even arrived in Canada, I became one of the vulnerable people harmed by the system itself.

I used to believe in one thing: that if I worked hard enough, the world would at least respond to that effort — not always with success, not always with fairness, but at least with cause and effect. If I studied hard, I would earn results. If I worked diligently, I would gain experience. If I made up for my weaknesses, I would move a little closer to my goal. That belief carried me for years. It was what kept me moving forward even when I was exhausted.

To apply for a Master's program in design in Canada, I spent a year and a half preparing. I paid 100,000 RMB to attend a design bootcamp — working during the day, building my portfolio at night, and spending weekends revising design projects and studying design theory, refining my portfolio piece by piece. At the same time, I had to prepare for the IELTS exam. Under enormous pressure, I memorized vocabulary and did practice tests every day, and sat the exam three times in total. Before every exam, I was too anxious to sleep, but I still walked into the test room each time — because I believed that if I could overcome these obstacles, my path to studying in Canada would finally open.

During my bootcamp training, my instructor told me that design education in Canada doesn't only care about whether an interface looks good — it cares about whether design can genuinely respond to people's real circumstances: social issues, vulnerable populations, public systems, human dignity. That gave me something to look forward to. I finally felt I could discover the real value of design — that in Canada I would learn how to use the power of design to help society, and how to make systems more understanding of people.

The irony is that I had not even set foot in Canada before I became one of the vulnerable people myself — crushed by an unfeeling bureaucratic system.

I am simply someone who studied Advertising as an undergraduate and has spent six years working as a UI/UX designer. Yet, for no clear reason, my study permit application was thrown into security screening. There was no clear explanation, no defined timeframe, and no one I could actually speak to. I don't know where the suspicion came from, and I don't know what exactly I am supposed to prove. I did nothing wrong, and yet I have been held back for more than a year by a suspicion I cannot even see.

Over the past year, I have tried nearly every reasonable, legal, and legitimate channel available. I have submitted webforms. I have looked into GCMS records, ATIP requests, and inquiries to CBSA and CSIS. I have pursued a Writ of Mandamus. As an ordinary applicant, I have exhausted every rule available to me.

But no one seems to care. Every webform is answered with the same template response. When and how much of my file gets disclosed to me is entirely outside my control. The Mandamus process has dragged on for a long time without truly pulling me out of this black box. The whole process seems designed to tell me: you may submit, you may wait, you may feel anxious, you may fall apart — but they have no obligation to respond to you.

Even though I have done nothing wrong, I still have to accept the punishment.

I have followed every rule, yet this system has never once truly respected me. It demands documents, fees, time, explanations, and patience from me, while giving me nothing substantive in return. It can consume a full year of my life, disrupt my studies, my career, my relationships, and my mental health — without ever having to explain to me: why me? How much longer? What else can I possibly do?

There have been countless times I lost control of my emotions, and countless times I simply went numb. Many times, I genuinely wanted to write down exactly what I felt and send it through the webform, to ask them plainly: Why? Why should someone who has worked this hard be shelved indefinitely, over a suspicion whose origin no one will explain? Why should a country that claims to respect democracy, the rule of law, and human rights leave a lawful applicant with no meaningful channel to communicate? Why must I remain restrained, polite, and compliant at all times, while the system is allowed to stay silent, stall, and copy-paste the same reply, again and again?

But in the end, I held myself back, again and again. I still chose to follow their rules — because I was afraid that any genuine expression of anger might be turned against me and used as evidence to hurt my case.

I have come to realize, more and more clearly, that the machinery of the state does not always operate as nobly as it claims. Often the logic is much simpler than that: is there a cost, is there pressure, is there something at stake. We, as foreigners, have no vote, no political power, and little public visibility. We pay our visa fees, our tuition deposits; we give up our time, our energy, our hopes, and our life plans — yet when we are set aside, no one truly seems to be held accountable.

I came to this with a sincere wish: to learn how design could help society, how it could make systems fairer and more humane. But before I had even arrived, a system had already proven something to me — that once you become the one who is overlooked, it becomes almost impossible to even make others see your pain. I discovered, with despair, that everything I am good at — effort, proof, explanation, revision, communication — has no entry point in the face of an opaque system. I once wanted to use design to help more people. Now, I have even begun to doubt whether I will ever get the chance to do that.

Because right now, I have discovered that I cannot even save myself.

Lili Liang
