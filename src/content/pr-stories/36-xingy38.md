# 34 Months in Limbo: Trapped by a Closed Work Permit During Security Screening

In mid-2023, I applied for permanent residence in Canada through a **Provincial Nominee Program (PNP)**, believing I was beginning a clear and structured path toward building my future here. I passed eligibility, medical examinations, and criminality checks. I lawfully obtained a work permit, moved to Canada, secured employment, paid taxes, and began building a life in my community. At every stage, I complied fully with the rules.

In early 2024, my family’s files entered **security screening**. Since then, there have been no decisions and no explanation. What began as a structured, rules-based pathway has become an **indefinite administrative pause**. A process that once progressed predictably has stalled without a timeline, clarity, or meaningful communication.

The application has now been pending for about **34 months**, including **more than 23 months in security screening alone**.

During this entire period, I have been working under an **employer-specific (closed) work permit**. This type of permit ties a worker to a single employer and has effectively locked my career to one employer.

Over the past few years, Canada’s economic conditions have become more challenging. Businesses are facing increasing pressure, including my employer. At the same time, the **cost of living continues to rise**. However, under a closed work permit, I have had to decline stronger employment opportunities—not because of performance or qualifications, but because administrative restrictions leave no flexibility. Nor can I take on additional work to supplement my income.

The **cost of delay is cumulative, concrete, and irreversible**. Each year spent in uncertainty represents lost opportunities for career growth, financial stability, and long-term planning. Over time, the strain has taken a real toll on my family. The laughter that once filled our home has gradually faded, replaced by days overshadowed by anxiety and quiet exhaustion.

In my own experience, I have come to embrace what **Mark Carney** once expressed:

> “We take the world as it is, not as we wish it to be.”

Security screening is part of that reality, and I have accepted it. I respect the importance of security screening for Canada’s national interests and remain fully willing to cooperate with the process.

But as Carney has also emphasized:

> “Canada is a stable and reliable partner in a world that is anything but.”

Within that spirit of predictability, it is reasonable to hope that the security screening process can also provide a **clearer timeline, a reliable roadmap, and a fair and transparent procedure free from nationality-based prejudice**, so that individuals like me can plan our next steps and our future with greater confidence.

When permanent residence applications experience prolonged delays—especially during security screening—it can be extremely difficult for applicants who remain tied to **employer-specific work permits** that limit their ability to sustain themselves and their families.

Under the current rules for **Bridging Open Work Permits (BOWP)**, provincial nominees must **“have no employment restrictions as a condition of their nomination.”** Because my nomination includes employment restrictions, I remain bound to a single employer while my permanent residence application continues to be processed.

Given the length of the delay, I sincerely hope there might be **some flexibility** that would allow applicants in situations like mine to obtain a work permit with greater mobility while still respecting the provincial nomination framework.

In practical terms, this would simply mean allowing an **open work permit within the nominating province**, without tying the applicant to a single employer. Such flexibility would allow us to maintain our livelihoods, support our families, and continue contributing to Canada while waiting for the process to conclude.
